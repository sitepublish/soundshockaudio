Loudness standards in audio refer to standardized measurements and targets for perceived loudness, developed to ensure consistent playback levels across different media and platforms. The International Telecommunication Union's BS.1770 recommendation established the LUFS (Loudness Units Full Scale) measurement standard, which accounts for human perception of loudness across different frequencies using K-weighting. The European Broadcasting Union's R128 standard specifies -23 LUFS for broadcast content, while the ATSC A/85 standard in the United States targets -24 LKFS (equivalent to LUFS). Major streaming platforms adopted loudness normalization: Spotify normalizes to approximately -14 LUFS, Apple Music to -16 LUFS, YouTube to -14 LUFS, and Tidal to -14 LUFS. These normalization practices have effectively ended the loudness war by removing the competitive advantage of excessively compressed masters, as overly loud tracks are simply turned down by the platform. True peak measurement, which detects inter-sample peaks that can cause distortion during format conversion, has become an essential complement to LUFS-based loudness targeting.

Equalization (EQ) is the process of adjusting the balance between frequency components within an electronic audio signal. The concept originated in early telephone networks where equalization circuits compensated for signal loss over long cable runs. In music production, EQ is used to shape the tonal character of individual tracks and the overall mix. Parametric EQ provides continuous control over center frequency, bandwidth (Q), and gain, making it the most versatile type for mixing applications. Graphic EQ divides the spectrum into fixed frequency bands with individual level controls. Shelving EQ boosts or cuts all frequencies above or below a specified frequency. High-pass and low-pass filters remove frequencies outside a desired range. The human audible spectrum extends from approximately 20 Hz to 20 kHz, and effective equalization requires understanding how different instruments and sounds occupy specific regions within this range.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a MIDI controller?
A MIDI controller is a hardware device that sends MIDI data to your DAW, typically featuring piano-style keys, pads, knobs, and faders. It does not produce sound on its own but triggers virtual instruments and controls parameters in your software. Popular options include the Akai MPK Mini, Novation Launchkey, and Arturia KeyLab series.
What is the difference between VST and AU plugins?
VST (Virtual Studio Technology) is a cross-platform format developed by Steinberg, while AU (Audio Units) is Apple's native plugin format exclusive to macOS. Logic Pro only supports AU plugins, while most other DAWs on Mac support both formats. Functionally, the audio quality is identical between formats.
What is reverb and when should I use it?
Reverb simulates the natural reflections of sound in a physical space, adding depth and dimension to dry recordings. Use shorter reverb times for intimate, close sounds and longer times for epic, spacious atmospheres. SoundShockAudio features several highly-rated free reverb plugins that rival paid alternatives.
