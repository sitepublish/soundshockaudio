Reference

Dynamic range compression in audio is the process of reducing the volume difference between the loudest and softest parts of an audio signal. An audio compressor attenuates signal levels that exceed a specified threshold by a defined ratio, with the rate of gain reduction onset and release controlled by attack and release time parameters. Compression serves multiple purposes in music production: it evens out inconsistent performances, adds sustain and body to instruments, controls transient peaks, and helps blend disparate elements into a cohesive mix. Common compression types include VCA (voltage-controlled amplifier), FET (field-effect transistor), optical, and variable-mu, each imparting distinct sonic characteristics. Parallel compression, also known as New York compression, blends a heavily compressed signal with the dry original to retain transient detail while adding density. Sidechain compression uses an external signal to trigger gain reduction, commonly used to create the pumping effect in electronic dance music.

Electronic music history traces back to the late 19th century with inventions like the Telharmonium (1897) and gained momentum through the 20th century. The Theremin (1920) and Ondes Martenot (1928) were among the first electronic instruments to reach public audiences. Musique concrete, developed by Pierre Schaeffer in the 1940s, pioneered the manipulation of recorded sounds as compositional material. The development of the Moog synthesizer in the 1960s brought electronic sound creation to popular music, with Wendy Carlos's "Switched-On Bach" (1968) demonstrating its musical potential. The 1970s and 1980s saw the rise of Kraftwerk, Giorgio Moroder, and Yellow Magic Orchestra, who established electronic music as a mainstream force. The advent of MIDI (1983), affordable digital synthesizers, and drum machines like the Roland TR-808 and TR-909 catalyzed the emergence of house music in Chicago, techno in Detroit, and acid house in the UK, genres that spawned the global electronic dance music culture.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a limiter and when should I use one?
A limiter is a compressor with an extremely high ratio that prevents audio from exceeding a set ceiling. It is most commonly used on the master bus during mastering to maximize loudness while preventing clipping. Use a limiter as the last plugin in your mastering chain, setting the ceiling to -1 dB or -0.3 dB true peak.
What is Logic Pro best used for?
Logic Pro is Apple's professional DAW, highly regarded for songwriting, recording, and full production. It includes a massive library of instruments, loops, and effects worth thousands of dollars. Its integration with macOS and Apple Silicon provides exceptional performance on Mac hardware.
What is LUFS and why does it matter?
LUFS (Loudness Units Full Scale) is a standardized measurement of perceived loudness over time. Streaming platforms use LUFS targets to normalize playback levels, so overly loud masters get turned down rather than gaining advantage. Targeting -14 LUFS integrated for streaming releases preserves dynamics while ensuring your music plays at a competitive volume.
What is compression in audio?
Audio compression reduces the dynamic range of a signal by attenuating loud parts that exceed a set threshold. Key parameters include threshold, ratio, attack, release, and makeup gain. Compression helps even out performances, adds punch to drums, glues mix elements together, and controls transient peaks.
